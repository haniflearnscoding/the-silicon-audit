Design System

The visual language of The Silicon Audit is built from three sources: the editorial authority of print journalism, the analytical clarity of Information is Beautiful, and the intimate unit-mark vocabulary of Dear Data.

Primary Visual Influence · Data Visualization

Information
is Beautiful

David McCandless's foundational principle — that area should be proportional to value — directly shapes every visualization in The Silicon Audit. His use of clean geometric forms (circles, bubbles) to encode magnitude makes the Reality Gap™ viscerally legible without text explanation. The IIB aesthetic also informs the site's confident use of color as semantic signal rather than decoration.

Primary Visual Influence · Personal Data / Unit Marks

Dear Data

Giorgia Lupi and Stefanie Posavec's year-long postcard project — now in MoMA's permanent collection — establishes the unit-mark vocabulary used throughout The Silicon Audit. Each mark represents a discrete real-world event. The hand-drawn quality of the originals is reproduced in code via SVG feTurbulence + feDisplacementMap filters, which introduce controlled visual noise that mimics the organic imprecision of hand drawing — making data feel human-scale and personal rather than algorithmic.

dear-data.com ↗

"Every week, we measured a particular type of data about our lives — and used those numbers to make a drawing on a postcard. One mark equals one moment."
— Giorgia Lupi & Stefanie Posavec

The principle

One mark = one real-world event. A broken circle = one missed deadline. Eleven broken circles = eleven missed deadlines. No aggregation, no abstraction.

The translation

SVG feTurbulence at baseFrequency=".045" displaces each mark ±1.5px — reproducing the organic hand-drawn texture in code.

MoMA collection

The original 104 postcards were acquired by the Museum of Modern Art in November 2016 as part of the Architecture & Design permanent collection.

Core Design Principles
01
Area ∝ Value

Visual encoding uses area, not length. A circle ten times larger = a value ten times larger. Following the IIB principle: ink should map honestly to data. No distortion, no truncated axes.

02
One Mark, One Event

Every symbol in a unit chart represents exactly one real-world occurrence — one broken promise, one documented error, one missed deadline. Density of marks = density of reality.

03
Color is Semantic

Company colors (OAI green, Meta blue) appear only for that company's data. Red = observed/actual. Amber = disputed. Color carries meaning — it is never used decoratively.

04
Satire + Rigor

Tone is sharp and satirical, but every figure is a calculation with cited, verifiable sources. The distressed rubber stamps and bureaucratic framing amplify — not undermine — the data's authority.
