01  Abstract & Paper

The Reality Gap™

How do you visualize a lie that doesn't know it's lying? This project proposes a systematic, data-rigorous approach to accountability journalism in the technology sector.

The technology industry operates under a peculiar epistemic norm: executives are rewarded for projecting certainty, investors prize conviction over calibration, and the press cycle has a memory of approximately eleven days. The result is a near-total absence of institutional accountability for public statements that turn out to be false.

This project, The Silicon Audit, proposes a data visualization framework for systematically documenting the gap between public claims made by technology executives and observable outcomes — a quantity I term the Reality Gap™. Rather than relying on subjective editorial judgment, the Reality Gap™ is a precise mathematical measure: the percentage deviation between a stated projection and the observed outcome at the stated deadline.

The central design challenge is representing data that is simultaneously quantitative and rhetorical. The numbers are exact, but the story they tell is about human overconfidence, institutional incentives, and the structural conditions that allow misleading statements to go uncorrected. The visualization must be rigorous enough to withstand academic scrutiny while legible enough to carry genuine satirical and journalistic force.

Drawing on the visual languages of Information is Beautiful (McCandless) and Dear Data (Lupi & Posavec), I propose a web-based "Bureaucratic Museum" — a newspaper-formatted feed of claim records, each accompanied by a bespoke data visualization that encodes the Reality Gap™ through proportional areas, unit marks, and annotated dot plots rather than conventional charts.

The project makes three contributions: (1) a replicable methodology for auditing executive claims, (2) a typology of visualization strategies suited to data about performance certainty versus actual performance, and (3) a public-facing interface that demonstrates how data journalism can carry both epistemic and aesthetic weight.

Research Questions
1
How can proportional area and unit-mark visualization strategies encode the scale of executive overstatement more effectively than conventional time-series charts?
2
What editorial and typographic conventions from print journalism can be repurposed to give data visualization work the authority of institutional accountability?
3
Can satirical tone and rigorous methodology coexist in a single public-facing artifact without undermining either?
