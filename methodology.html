The Silicon Audit — Methodology & Standards

How We Measure

The Reality Gap™ is not an opinion. It is a calculation. Here is exactly how we perform it, how we classify claims, and how we handle edge cases, retractions, and appeals.

I. Claim Eligibility

Not every statement made by a technology executive qualifies for audit. We apply a strict four-part eligibility test before any claim enters the database.

Eligibility Criteria

  1. Specificity
    The claim must contain a measurable outcome. Vague aspirations ("we want to make the world better") are excluded. Testable assertions ("we will reach 100M users by Q2") are eligible. If the claim cannot be falsified, it cannot be audited.
  2. Public Record
    The claim must appear in a verifiable primary source: an earnings call transcript, a public keynote, a regulatory filing, a signed press release, or a verified social media post from an official account. Off-the-record statements and anonymous sourcing are excluded.
  3. Implied or Stated Deadline
    There must be a timeframe against which the claim can be evaluated. "Within 12 months," "by end of year," "in the next decade," and specific calendar dates all qualify. Open-ended statements without any temporal anchor are deferred, not excluded — they may become eligible if a reasonable horizon passes.
  4. Materiality
    The claim must be significant enough that its failure would constitute meaningful information for the public, investors, regulators, or consumers. Minor operational statements are excluded. Claims about core products, revenue trajectories, safety characteristics, or technological capabilities are eligible.

II. The Reality Gap™ Formula

Once a claim passes the eligibility test and its deadline has passed, we compute the Reality Gap™. This is a standardised measure of the proportional distance between the stated claim and the observed outcome.

Reality Gap™ — Core Formula (v2.1)
RG = ( O − P ) / | P | × 100
Where: O = Observed outcome at stated deadline · P = Projected/claimed value · RG = Reality Gap™ (%)
Negative values indicate underdelivery. Positive values indicate overdelivery (rare).
For timeline claims: RG = (Actual completion date − Stated deadline) in days, annualised.

Worked Example

Claim: "Horizon Worlds will reach 500,000 daily active users by Q4 2022."
Observed: ~200,000 DAU at peak, Q4 2022.
RG = (200,000 − 500,000) / |500,000| × 100 = −60%

Note: the Meta filing uses a longer projection (1B by 2031) which, when annualised against 2022 trajectory, produces the −99.6% figure. Both calculations are preserved in the source file.

III. Claim Classification

Every audited claim receives one of four designations, assigned by a majority vote of the Audit Committee after independent verification by at least two members.

Designation Badge Criteria RG Threshold
Retracted RETRACTED Claim is unambiguously falsified by the observable record. No reasonable interpretation allows the claim to be considered accurate. RG < −40%
Partial PARTIAL Claim contains elements that are verifiable but also elements that are not. Or the outcome falls within a grey zone where interpretation is legitimately contested. −40% ≤ RG < −10%
Disputed DISPUTED The claim's accuracy is contested by two or more credible sources reaching different conclusions from the same underlying data. The Audit Committee does not assign a verdict pending resolution. Contested data
Substantiated SUBSTANTIATED Claim is supported by independent, verifiable evidence that meets our standard of proof. This designation is rare by design — we apply a higher burden of proof to verification than to falsification. RG ≥ −10%

Committee Voting

Classification requires a majority (3 of 4) vote from the Audit Committee. In the event of a 2–2 split, the claim is automatically classified as DISPUTED until one member changes position or new evidence is submitted. No single editor can unilaterally assign or change a classification.

IV. Source Standards

All claims are cross-referenced against a minimum of two independent primary sources before entering the database. For the observed outcome, we require at least one of the following:

Tier 1 Sources (highest weight)

SEC or regulatory filings · Sworn congressional testimony · Peer-reviewed academic publications · Independently audited financial statements · Court documents

Tier 2 Sources (standard weight)

Quarterly earnings call transcripts (official) · Signed press releases · Verified executive social media · Major wire service reporting (Reuters, AP, Bloomberg) with named sources

Tier 3 Sources (corroborating only)

Trade press · Analyst reports · Industry surveys · Investigative reporting with anonymous sources. Tier 3 sources may be used to contextualise but never as the sole basis for a classification.

Every source used in a filing is archived by us at the time of publication. If a source is subsequently deleted, altered, or paywalled, our archived copy serves as the record of what it contained. Source links and archive references are available in full on each claim page.

V. Corrections Policy

Corrections are published at the top of the original filing, not appended to the bottom. The original incorrect text is preserved with a strikethrough and the correction is clearly labelled with a date. We do not silently edit filings.

If a classification changes after new evidence emerges, the previous classification is also preserved in the filing's version history, which is publicly accessible. We consider the practice of quietly updating past statements — common in the industry we cover — to be beneath the standards of any publication that claims to value accuracy.

Anyone, including the subjects of our filings, may submit a correction request at corrections@siliconaudit.press. Correction requests must include: the specific claim disputed, the specific error alleged, and at minimum one Tier 1 or Tier 2 source supporting the correction. We aim to respond within 10 business days.

VI. Scope & Limitations

The Reality Gap™ measures what was said against what happened. It does not measure intent, competence, or moral culpability. A claim that turns out to be wrong because of genuinely unforeseeable external circumstances receives the same mathematical Reality Gap™ as one that was known to be false when made. We make note of context in the filing text, but we do not adjust the RG figure.

We also acknowledge that prediction is genuinely hard. Technology timelines are notoriously difficult to forecast. The Silicon Audit does not hold executives to a standard of perfect foresight. We hold them to the standard of their own public words — and specifically to the habit of issuing public statements with the rhetorical confidence of certainty rather than the epistemic humility of estimation.

If a CEO says "we believe this might be achievable by end of year, conditions permitting," that is hedged and would not qualify as an auditable claim under our specificity criterion. If the same CEO says "this will happen by end of year — I'm certain of it," that is a different statement and is auditable.

The gap we are measuring is not between prediction and reality. It is between performed certainty and reality. That is the specific rhetorical pattern that The Silicon Audit was built to document.
